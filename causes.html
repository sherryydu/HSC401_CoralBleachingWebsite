Summary

  • Corals are super sensitive to changes in temperature, oxygen, and light.
  • Warmer oceans damage zooxanthellae and stress corals out.
  • Leaking fertilizers stress corals by speeding up zooxanthellae growth.
  • Some fishing practices increase seaweed populations, which shade out coral.
  • Some sunscreen chemicals block the sun's rays and may make corals vulnerable to viruses.

Climate Change

When temperatures are high, the tiny plants (called zooxanthellae) living inside corals becomes damaged. This means they are no longer useful to the coral. On top of that, corals can't handle temperature changes of more than 1°C. To cope, corals will kick these zooxanthellae out, but the damage is already done.

Fisheries

Some fisheries leave non-specific traps or nets in the water. The real catch is, when they hoist them up, they may eliminate seaweed-eating fish from coral reef environments. Without these fish, seaweed populations can bloom. These seaweed steal all the sun's rays and oxygen in the water, which inevitably suffocates the corals.

Farming

Farmers may use fertilizers to give their crops some extra oomph. However, these chemicals can make zooxanthellae grow fast if they leak into the water. Like an overworked chef, corals can't keep up with this hungry crowd's demands for a certain type of food (Phosphorus). After too much stress, corals will kick them to the curb.

Sunscreen

Every time you go into the ocean with sunscreen, a common sunscreen chemical called oxybenzone washes off your skin into the water. Just like oxybenzone blocks the sun's rays on your skin, it does the same to coral. If this continues, then the coral will become infected with viruses and induce coral bleaching.
